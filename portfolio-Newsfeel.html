How can we encourage empathy and prosocial dialogue about current events?

Project Details

When - Spring 2020

Duration - Approx. 8 weeks

Course - Social Web, Carnegie Mellon University

Skills - Interaction + UX design

Teammates - Aaron Bishop, Alice Li, Laura Fulton, Dana Frostig

Role - Lead Designer

Scope

Since the rise of social media networks in the late 2000's, the world has become connected in new and novel ways. This increased connectivity has lead users to seek out information, friends, and social networks that most align with their beliefs and interests. This has caused major problems nationally and internationally due to the rise of increasing partisan beliefs, a growing lack of empathy, and social based "filter bubbles". This project attempts to remedy these problems by creating a mobile application for dialogue and many view points about current events.

Dialogue between individuals with different perspectives on current events has devolved and become toxic online.

User's lack diverse opinions outside of their social "filter bubbles"

There is a growing lack of empathy towards differing opinions

Digital social circles tend to be echo chambers of beliefs and ideas

User's lack access to unpartisan media and content when making their own beliefs

Solution: NewsFeel - A mobile application that allows users to look at current events from multiple perspectives and discuss their beliefs in diverse friendly chatrooms.

Newsfeel gives users the ability to browse and read about current event topics from a range of media sources. After reading top news stories, users can choose to like and dislike them based on their opinions and then do a quick followup questionnaire. These results can then be fed into a chat now function which guides users into friendly chat rooms for more personal dialogue promoting empathetic behavior.

Problem space: A majority of users read about current events online and through social media - this leads to biased and filtered perspectives.

Through the use of guerrilla research and directed storytelling, we found that a majority of users get their news from social media platforms via their smart devices. Those who self curate their current event digest, find it increasingly challenging to break from their preferred new sources like the New York Times and Fox news due to confirmation bias. Users feel trapped in their trusted news sources and struggle to find diverse opinions to help them empathize with people who have different beliefs.

“I curate my own news content... it means I have to go out of my way for diverse opinions.”

“There are certain news sources I would never read... I’d rather not waste my time with articles I do not agree with.”

“Sometimes I feel like I live in two separate worlds on Facebook between the Fox and NYT articles... What happened?”

Current solutions do exist to help with bias and filter bubbles but they focus too much on politics and are not mobile centric.

Most of the product solutions that exist look at exposing bias and political leaning rather then increasing empathy and dialogue. While viewing users own bias can be powerful for identity and transparency into oneself, it is alone not powerful to help users break from their filter bubbles and empathize. Not only that, most products that do exist today work as either google chrome plugins, standalone news curation websites, or facebook plugins. Our solution will be mobile friendly and leech on users browsing data for bias detection.

Storyboarding lead us to realize the importance of having a inclusive and fun experience. Specifically including access to chatrooms

By using storyboarding as a medium to discuss possible solutions with users, it seemed pretty obvious that no new product would be adopted unless it was fun an d casual. Many users preferred our ideas that utilized chatrooms and reddit like browsing. One storyboard stood out by combining news browsing and rating articles - with the interaction of swiping (based on tinder). With these insights we moved into exploring chatrooms and shaping the product further.

To design the perfect chatroom for empathetic dialogue - we created our own chatroom experience with a helpful chat bot to facilitate. Users loved it

With our product form beginning to take shape, we wanted to test a core principle of our application, that is "can a diverse anonymous chatroom about news stories encourage empathetic dialogue?" To do this, we selected four diverse participants and gave them 3 different articles about Covid-19: one from Fox news, one from the BBC, and one from NPR. From there, we let them join the chat room and I acted like a chat bot to facilitate the conversation for the next half hour.

The Newsfeel Chat Bot Study

Overall, the dialogue between user subjects was mostly light, fun, and poked at how surreal society was due to Covid-19. While specific facts from the articles were not addressed by participants, it was apparent that the information made a difference in the exchange of ideas and story telling. After about 20 minutes in the study, certain participants began to open up about how personally challenging things were where they lived and for their families. Below are quotes from users taken after the study was completed.

“Porky really changed my perspective.. since I have lived in the city my whole life it's hard to imagine the effects in rural areas”

“The articles helped frame the discussion... b0ssbabe made an effort to try to be inclusive in her views because of it”

“Talking about it (covid-19) made me feel better... even if we didn't agree on everything it was nice to just decompress”

Hand sketching the flow of the app helped me visualize a customer's journey towards empathy and dialogue with strangers

As the lead designer on the team, I began to sketch on how users journeys would begin. From the moment they open the app, to the second a topic catches their eye, to the (hopefully) the life changing conversation they would have in the chat room, it should all be part of the experience. The product itself would be a manifestation of the empathic experience going from awareness, to self education, to finally dialogue.

Lo-fi prototyping and early user testing helped me to understand how users want to onboard into chatrooms

With an overall flow beginning to take shape from topic selection to chatroom, I wanted to test it with users. Through this a few crucial pieces of feedback emerged. First, I learned that the article swiping caused heuristic issues and users preferred a more thumbs up/ down approach (incase they wanted to change their minds). Testing also reveled that users wanted at max 3 articles before entering a chat room due to attention span creep. Lastly, that a preview page was crucial at the home browse screen for when users selected a topic. This is because large topics like "global warming" can consist of large number of subtopics.

Creating a mood board for our product based on warm inviting tones and contemporary colors

With a more refined flow beginning to take shape after user testing, I set about creating an mood board and design style guide for the visuals in the project. Inspired by European furniture designers and contemporary colors, I created a mood board focused on inviting users with high contrast colors to stand out against the many news articles linked on the application. The warm tones would also add more friendly colors to the chat functionality.

Mid-fi prototyping lead to increased visual functionality and new reflection feature

Building off of the style guide I created, I began to update the overall look to reflect the colors and fonts in the application. I also began to explore this notion of a "reflection" page. On this page, users could reflect on their articles and conversations and write a blurb to save for late. This would serve as a guide to help user remember previous discussions and to note down any cathartic moments they experience.

Hi-Fi product screens

The final high fidelity product screens were created as part of the deliverable for the project and used as a guide during the presentation to faculty in the HCI Institute at Carnegie Mellon University. The final screens were a useful exercise in animation and expressing digital product experiences.

Browse and topic selection

When users open the application, the first thing that pops up are suggested topic cards for users to explore. They can choose to select these major topic cards or use the filters on top of the page (based on reddit’s hot, top, and new).

Exploring articles in a topic

Once a topic is selected, users can swipe between the 3 different articles about the topic. The articles load within the app and can be liked, disliked, or ignored.

News diversity quiz

After users have completed reading the articles, they will be prompted with sliding scale questions relating to the topic. These questions asks users things like "how much does this personally effect you" and "how important is this topic". Through the answers of these questions, the system will use the data to pair you into a diverse chat room if you choose to proceed.

News quiz and article breakdown

Once the quiz is completed, users will see how their responses ranked in comparison to other users of the application. It will also show hoe many other users liked or disliked the articles in the topic. Users at this point can write a reflection, choose a new topic, or join a chat room.

Joining a chatroom

If users decide to join a chat room, they will be prompted to write a quick bio about why they want to join and can choose how to display their user name. To help break the ice, a chat bot will help to facilitate the conversation and other users bios can be read. F

Writing a reflection

Once users finish a chatroom session or topic articles, they can jump into leaving a message for themselves to read later about the experience. These reflections are stored in the profile page for later to be reviewed if desired.

What comes next for Newsfeel? Test, build and refine our chatroom algorithm

The project was considered a success by HCI faculty and the professors of the Social Web course. We were lauded for attempting to tackle such a challenging problem space and professors suggested we explore quicker ways to jump into informed chatrooms beyond making users read articles. Our next steps for the project are to explore faster simpler ways to create diverse chat rooms and work on creating an algorithm to work as a filter for the chatrooms.
