Public art is mostly ignored. How can we make it our own?

Project Details

When - Fall 2019

Duration - Approx. 12 weeks

Course - User Centered Research and Evaluation, Carnegie Mellon University

Skills - UX Research + UX design

Teammates - Olivia Li, Alice Li, and Anurati Sodani

Scope

Metro 21 teamed up with Carnegie Mellon University to create digital assets in the city of Pittsburgh around public art sites. This study was conducted to shed some light on ways which digital technology could augment users experiences with art while generating analytic data for the city to use in future art endeavors. Our class was brought in to continue the next phase of the work by creating a digital asset to further the data generation.

Problem

Art Goes unnoticed by locals and toursits

Lack of awareness for public art

Disconnect in identity

General uninterest in static art

Solution: Make Art your own with AR!

Our solution is an augmented reality (AR) platform that encourages interaction between a user, the art piece and the wider community. In the AR space, users can interact with public art by posting comments, adding emojis and viewing other community members’ posts. This is reinforced by placing a sign by the physical art to inform the public of the potential for interaction.

Process

For this project being part of a research class - our structure was rigid in terms of design process. The flow would consist starting with back ground research, observe and intercept study, guiding into infinity diagraming, think-aloud studies, speed dating + story boarding, experience prototyping, and final design poster showcasing your solution. Some of these phases are represented below.

Affinity Mapping + Observe and Intercept

To understand the problem space further and gain insights into how we could increase art engagement - we conducted an observe and intercept session in Downtown Pittsburgh. During that session we spoke with 6 people who each represented a different demographic of user. After that - we preformed an affinity diagramming session. This lead us to realize that there are opportunities to increase the branding, identity, and understanding of public art in PGH.

Think-Aloud Study

With a better understanding of the problem space, we moved into seeing if a current solution existed on the market that could be leveraged to solve our problem. After searching we agreed that yelp offered some functionality that could potentially solve our stakeholders need for engagement while creating a more holistic public art experience in PGH. We developed a script and multiple actions for users to preform including finding public art, learning about it, creating a list of art to see, and sharing it with their friends.

Storyboarding and Speed-dating concepts

Reflecting on our insights and knowledge gained from our TAP study- we looked for areas to ideate on that could solve our core stakeholder need of improving engagement and also improve the users experience through identity, understanding, and interaction. With that in mind we created 12 different story boards to use during speed dating sessions. The storyboards were divided into 4 sections: identity, information, increasing awareness, and way-finding. Identity ideas - specifically a AR solution which incorporated users leaving notes and messages to the art - was the most popular. These are he story board concepts I drew for the project.

Experience Prototyping

With the direction decided through the storyboarding process - we formulated a way to test the concept and conduct an experience prototype session. Our idea was to create an artificial piece of public art and place it in a popular square in Pittsburgh. Then users could interact with our clear plastic screens representing the AR mode on a phone. Users then could choose to either leave a message in AR space, place an emoji on the art piece, or just browse what other people said about the work.

Solution - Make Art your own!

Our solution is an augmented reality (AR) platform that encourages interaction between a user, the art piece and the wider community. In the AR space, users can interact with public art by posting comments, adding emojis and viewing other community members’ posts. This is reinforced by placing a sign by the physical art to inform the public of the potential for interaction. To track the level of engagement, we will measure the number of posts and active users in the app. With user-generated data, the cost of public art can be justified by analyzing the posts and quantifying the success of individual art pieces. We will partner with corporations and social media platforms for advertising and leveraging existing technology.

Users first will be prompted to download/ open the app by signage near the art. Then they will use the qr code to enter the "space" and be able to view the art piece comments through their screen

Users then can use emojis or messages to decorate, comment on, or just leave notes to the area for others to read

An example screen of how the art piece might look after a few people have interacted with it over the course of a day
