How can we provide more holistic service to sleep apnea patients?

Project Details

When - Spring 2020

Duration - Approx. 6 weeks

Course - Interaction Design Studio 2, Carnegie Mellon University

Skills - Interaction + UX design + Service Design

Teammates - Stephanie Wang, Cora Wang, and Lena Li

Scope

Sleep apnea is a problem

Sleep Apnea patients lack good service and treatment customization with HMEs. It leads to patients abandoning their treatments.

User's lack choice in mask customization and apnea products

HME's offer limited service to help apnea patients be comfortable

ResMed lacks channels to gain data about apnea mask usage

ResMed lacks services to directly sell sleep apnea products to customers

Solution: An AR application that enables customization and direct purchase of ResMed masks and accessories.

A responsive web dashboard for residential water customers and service providers that helps users visualizing their water usage, create water saving goals, and suggests tips to reduce usage.

Process

For this project being part of a studio class - our structure was rigid in terms of our design process. The flow would consist starting with back ground research, guiding into gorilla research, personas, journey mapping, wire framing, speed dating + story boarding, and three levels of design iteration. Some of these phases are represented below.

Gorilla research pointed to user's not understanding their water usage and provider's lacking tools to help customers

After conducting background research and scoping the project - our team reached out to local Pittsburgh water users, the city of PGH water authority, and city hall for gorilla research on the domain. These are some of their quotes that guided us.

“I don’t care about my water usage... Because I do not understand it!"

Local residential water customer

"What even is normal water usage for our city? How do we even implement sustainable water usage efforts?"

Member of Pittsburgh sustainable initiatives

"Most of our users could not tell you the difference between 10 gallons and 50 gallons .. that is a problem."

Service Provider from PGH water authority

Personas framed our ideation process

Using the gorilla research and contextual research - our team developed some personas to guide us through the UX process of what the stakeholders would best need.

Business persona - a city council member who encompasses the views of a pro sustainability platform

Provider persona - a senior customer service manager who wants to better customer relationship, reduce water waste, and reduce wasted water revenues.

Customer persona - a lower income student who struggles with not understanding water usage, with high water bills, and wants to reduce water usage.

Journey mapping led to gaps in the service provider's ecosystem

Customer Opportunity

Customers currently lack a way to tell if their strategies for conserving water are effective - in either the short or long term.

Provider Opportunity

Providers have no way of understanding customer water usage behaviors, system behaviors as a whole, and lack content to help users mitigate their water usage.

Storyboarding and Speed-dating led to a data-driven billing solution

Utilizing the needs we found through our previous processes - we created three different concepts to explore in storyboarding through the process of sped dating. A concept which helped users set goals and visual their water usage was the most popular.

Wireframing the billing platform

Our lo-fi mockup emphasized water usage through text, bar graphs and by training a machine learning model to understand usage behavior of users through prompting users with questions. This way the system could learn to understand user’s behaviors with water.

Cleaning up visuals and adding water reduction tips

The Mid-fi prototype was focused on cleaning up the visuals and adding new functionality to the design including a feature that allowed users to search and submit tips to reduce water usage by using a tag selection page. Another feature compared users water usage anonymously to other similar households to help give context of their usage. This comparison was to increase competitive drive to be lower than other families.

Hi-Fi Product: Provider View

The final provider interface reflected customer user pages. This being for two reasons: first, that when customers call providers for assistance, they can walk them through the web portal directly, and second, so that both the provider and customers have the access to the same sorts of data. The difference being for the providers is that they are able to see customers history in insights and able to see the customer’s billing history. Other functionality that the provider view has is the ability to search user's profiles directly, use a map to view any issues in customers properties or water districts, and update any notes on customers’ accounts.

Conclusion

We dubbed our final solution Maya Dashboard because Maya is the Hebrew name for water - and users/providers alike would be able to easily flow their data to understand water usage. Overall reception of the project was high and as a team we felt a great deal of success. Rather than approach the project from a more gamify or campy angle - we felt that by looking at the billing process our solution was pragmatic and applicable. The project helped me to understand the power of responsive web design as well as reinforced the overall UX design process. It also was a great exploration of figma's animation tools and Dreamweaver for final visualizations.
